Panchinala 2023: Monitoring Himalayan Glacier Health

July 15, 2023 Himalaya, Glaciology, Climate Change

Our research team returned to Panchinala Glacier in July 2023 for our annual monitoring expedition. Located in the western Himalayan region of India, the Panchinala Glacier has been one of our primary research sites since 2019. This visit marked our second consecutive year of comprehensive data collection following the pandemic interruption, allowing us to build a more complete picture of the glacier's response to changing climate conditions.

Research Objectives

This year's expedition had several key objectives:

  • Document changes in glacier terminus position compared to our 2021 baseline measurements
  • Measure mass balance using our established stake network
  • Collect ice and snow samples for isotopic analysis
  • Deploy additional autonomous weather stations at higher elevations
  • Conduct drone surveys to create updated digital elevation models

The Panchinala Glacier is particularly important to monitor as it feeds into the headwaters of rivers that supply water to millions of people downstream. Understanding its melt patterns and rate of retreat provides critical data for water resource management in the region.

Journey and Field Camp

Our team of six researchers departed from the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) in early July, traveling first to Manali and then undertaking a challenging two-day trek to reach our base camp at 4,200 meters elevation. The approach to Panchinala has become increasingly difficult over the years as the glacier retreats up-valley, requiring longer and more technical hiking routes.

We established our primary camp near the glacier terminus, with a satellite camp at 4,800 meters to facilitate work in the accumulation zone. Weather conditions were generally favorable during our two-week stay, with typical summer monsoon patterns bringing afternoon clouds and occasional precipitation, but mornings remained clear for fieldwork.

Findings and Observations

Our most significant observation this year was the accelerated retreat of the glacier terminus. Since our 2021 expedition, the terminus has retreated an average of 18.5 meters, which is approximately 20% faster than the historical rate we had documented in the previous decade. Several ice caves that were prominent features during our last visit have collapsed entirely, changing the morphology of the glacier front.

The stake network revealed concerning patterns in ice mass loss. Stakes positioned at lower elevations (below 4,400m) showed the most dramatic ice loss, with an average surface lowering of 2.7 meters over the past two years. This rate exceeds our previous measurements and suggests an acceleration in the thinning of the glacier.

Our drone surveys provided high-resolution imagery that, when compared with previous years' data, clearly visualizes the changing ice surface. Particularly notable was the expansion of supraglacial lakes and streams on the glacier surface, which can accelerate melt through increased absorption of solar radiation.

Local Community Engagement

This year, we made a concerted effort to engage with nearby villages that depend on meltwater from the Panchinala Glacier. We conducted three community workshops to share our findings and discuss the implications of glacier retreat for local water availability.

The conversations with community elders were particularly valuable, as they provided historical perspectives on the glacier that extend beyond our scientific records. Many described visible changes in the glacier and surrounding snowfields over their lifetimes, corroborating our measurements with generational knowledge.

We were also joined by two local students who assisted with our fieldwork as part of our educational outreach program. This initiative aims to build scientific capacity within the communities most affected by glacier changes, creating advocates for environmental monitoring and conservation.

Technical Innovations

This expedition marked our first deployment of compact, solar-powered weather stations designed specifically for high-altitude environments. These stations, developed in collaboration with engineers at IIT, can withstand extreme temperatures and heavy snowfall while collecting continuous data on temperature, precipitation, wind, and solar radiation.

We installed three of these stations at different elevations along the glacier, creating a vertical profile of meteorological conditions that will help us understand the microclimate factors affecting melt rates. The stations transmit data via satellite, allowing year-round monitoring without physical presence at the site.

Our drone survey techniques have also evolved, with improved flight planning and image processing workflows that generate more accurate digital elevation models. The comparison between this year's models and those from 2021 provides unprecedented detail on volumetric ice loss.

Challenges and Adaptations

Field research in high mountain environments always presents challenges, and this year was no exception. A significant rockfall event near our primary study area forced us to adjust our sampling locations and routes. This event itself was noteworthy, as it occurred in an area previously covered by ice, highlighting how glacier retreat can destabilize adjacent terrain.

Equipment malfunctions are inevitable in harsh conditions, and we lost one of our differential GPS units to water damage during a stream crossing. Fortunately, our redundant systems and adaptable methodology allowed us to continue data collection with minimal disruption.

Working in these environments teaches you to expect the unexpected. Flexibility and resourcefulness are as important as scientific expertise when conducting field glaciology.

Implications and Future Work

The data collected during this expedition paints a concerning picture of accelerated glacier decline. If current trends continue, our models suggest that the lower portions of Panchinala Glacier could disappear within 30-40 years, significantly earlier than previous projections.

This has profound implications for water security in the region, particularly during the pre-monsoon spring months when glacier melt provides a critical buffer before the arrival of seasonal precipitation. Communities and agricultural systems that have adapted to certain water availability patterns will face increasing challenges.

Our findings also contribute to the broader understanding of how Himalayan glaciers are responding to climate change. The Panchinala data will be integrated into regional hydrological models and climate assessments, helping refine predictions of future water availability across the Hindu Kush Himalayan region.

We plan to return to Panchinala in 2024 to continue our monitoring program, with an expanded focus on the hydrological outputs of the glacier system. By combining our glaciological measurements with stream discharge data, we hope to develop more precise models of how changes in ice mass translate to changes in water availability downstream.

As we packed up our camp and began the journey back to civilization, the receding ice of Panchinala Glacier stood as a stark reminder of our changing planet. Yet in the data we collected and the relationships we built with local communities, there is also hope that informed action can help societies adapt to the new realities of our mountain environments.
