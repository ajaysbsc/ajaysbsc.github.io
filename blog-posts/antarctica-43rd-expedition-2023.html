Antarctica 43rd Expedition: A First-Time Experience

February 10, 2023 Expedition, Research

In December 2022, I had the incredible opportunity to join the 43rd Indian Scientific Expedition to Antarctica, marking my first journey to the frozen continent. As a glaciologist specializing in ice dynamics, this expedition represented a pivotal moment in my research career and a chance to study firsthand one of Earth's most critical climate indicators.

The Journey South

Our expedition began with a long journey from Mumbai to Cape Town, South Africa, followed by a two-week voyage aboard the ice-class vessel MV Vasiliy Golovnin across the turbulent Southern Ocean. The notorious "Roaring Forties" and "Furious Fifties" latitudes lived up to their reputation, testing our sea legs and resilience before we even reached the ice.

The first sight of Antarctica's coastline is something I'll never forget – massive tabular icebergs floating majestically in the distance, and the pristine white landscape extending to the horizon. As we approached the Indian research station Bharati, located in the Larsemann Hills region of East Antarctica, the reality of working in such an extreme environment began to sink in.

Research Focus

My primary research objective during this expedition was to establish baseline measurements for several key glacial systems in the vicinity of the Indian station. Using a combination of ground-penetrating radar, differential GPS, and satellite imagery analysis, we aimed to quantify ice movement rates and thickness changes in response to regional climate patterns.

One of our most significant tasks involved setting up a network of stakes across the nearby glacier to track its movement over time. Each stake was precisely positioned using differential GPS, allowing for millimeter-level accuracy in measuring ice displacement. We also extracted several ice cores, which will provide valuable data on past climate conditions when analyzed back in our laboratory.

Life at the Station

Bharati Station, India's third research base in Antarctica, is a modern facility designed to withstand the harsh polar environment while providing comfortable living conditions for researchers. Despite being thousands of kilometers from home, the station offered surprising comforts – warm sleeping quarters, nutritious meals prepared by skilled chefs, and even occasional movie nights to maintain morale.

Daily life followed a structured routine, with early morning briefings to plan the day's activities based on weather conditions, followed by field trips or laboratory work. Safety was paramount, with strict protocols for all outdoor activities, including mandatory buddy systems and regular check-ins with the station commander.

The international nature of Antarctic research was evident through our interactions with researchers from other nations' bases. During my stay, we had the opportunity to visit the Russian station Progress and the Chinese station Zhongshan, exchanging scientific insights and fostering international cooperation in polar research.

Environmental Challenges

Working in Antarctica presents unique challenges unlike anywhere else on Earth. During our expedition, we experienced temperatures ranging from -5°C to -25°C, which while cold, were actually relatively mild for Antarctic summer conditions. The greater challenge came from the unpredictable weather patterns – katabatic winds could arise suddenly, reducing visibility to near zero and forcing immediate retreat to the safety of the station.

The constant daylight of the Antarctic summer disrupted our natural sleep cycles, requiring conscious effort to maintain regular rest periods. Physical exertion in the cold environment demanded significantly more energy than usual, and staying properly hydrated was a constant concern despite being surrounded by frozen water.

Scientific Discoveries

Our preliminary findings indicated concerning trends in the regional glaciers. We documented recession rates averaging 2.3 meters per year at the glacier fronts we monitored, consistent with warming trends observed across the Antarctic Peninsula. Surface melt features were more prevalent than expected based on historical records, suggesting increasing summer temperatures in the region.

One unexpected discovery was a previously unmapped subglacial lake beneath the eastern margin of our study area, detected through ground-penetrating radar surveys. This finding opens new research questions about the hydrological systems beneath East Antarctic glaciers and their potential influence on ice dynamics.

Personal Reflections

Beyond the scientific achievements, this expedition profoundly changed my perspective as a researcher and as a human being. The pristine beauty of Antarctica, with its otherworldly landscapes and untouched wilderness, reinforced the importance of preserving this unique environment for future generations.

The isolation and harsh conditions fostered deep bonds among expedition members, teaching valuable lessons about teamwork, resilience, and adaptation. Most importantly, witnessing firsthand the fragility of Earth's polar regions solidified my commitment to communicating the urgency of climate action through my research.

Antarctica doesn't just change your science; it changes your soul. Standing on ice that has existed for thousands of years humbles you and reminds you of our responsibility to protect this planet.

As I prepare for the 44th expedition next year, I carry with me not just the data we collected, but a deeper understanding of why this research matters. Antarctica's ice sheets hold the key to understanding our planet's climate future, and I am privileged to contribute to this crucial field of study.

Ajay Godara
